## Complications of Hypothyroidism

### Framing the Discussion

Complications of hypothyroidism fall into two broad categories:

1. **Complications of the disease itself** — what happens if hypothyroidism is undertreated, untreated, or unrecognized
2. **Complications of treatment** — adverse effects from levothyroxine replacement (essentially iatrogenic thyrotoxicosis) or complications arising in specific management contexts (post-thyroidectomy, post-RAI)

Understanding these complications requires returning to first principles: thyroid hormone regulates the basal metabolic rate of virtually every cell. When T4 is deficient, everything slows down and GAGs accumulate. When T4 is excessive (overreplacement), everything speeds up. The complications are therefore predictable from the physiology.

---

### Part A: Complications of Untreated or Undertreated Hypothyroidism

These are the consequences of prolonged thyroid hormone deficiency on each organ system. They can be thought of as the **natural history** of hypothyroidism if left unchecked.

#### 1. Cardiovascular Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Accelerated atherosclerosis and coronary artery disease** | ↓LDL receptor expression → ↑LDL-C → lipid deposition in arterial walls [5]; also ↑homocysteine (independent CVD risk factor); ↑diastolic BP (from ↑SVR) accelerates endothelial damage | Hypothyroidism is an independent risk factor for ischaemic heart disease. Importantly, treating the hypothyroidism may normalize the lipid profile without need for statins |
| **Diastolic hypertension** | ↓T4-mediated vasodilation → ↑systemic vascular resistance → ↑diastolic BP with normal or low systolic BP → narrow pulse pressure | Often overlooked; responds to thyroid hormone replacement |
| **Pericardial effusion** | GAG deposition in pericardial membranes → ↑capillary permeability → slow accumulation of protein-rich fluid in the pericardial space [1] | Usually develops slowly and is rarely haemodynamically significant (tamponade is uncommon); resolves with T4 replacement |
| ***Bradycardia / sick sinus syndrome*** | ↓Chronotropic effect of T4 on SA node pacemaker cells; ***hypothyroidism is an extrinsic cause of SSS*** [15] | Can cause fatigue, presyncope, and syncope; resolves with T4 replacement — avoid permanent pacemaker before treating thyroid |
| **Heart failure** | ↓Inotropy (contractility) + ↓chronotropy → ↓cardiac output; chronic volume overload from fluid retention; may develop dilated cardiomyopathy in severe, prolonged cases [29] | Usually reversible with T4 replacement; ***endocrine causes of DCMP include thyroid disease*** [29] |

**Why does hypothyroidism cause a pericardial effusion but rarely tamponade?** Because the effusion accumulates *very slowly* over weeks to months. The pericardial sac is compliant and stretches to accommodate gradual fluid accumulation. Tamponade occurs when fluid accumulates rapidly (e.g., trauma, acute pericarditis), exceeding the pericardium's ability to stretch. The slow pace of hypothyroid effusion allows adaptation.

#### 2. Metabolic and Biochemical Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology |
|---|---|
| ***Hyperlipidaemia (↑LDL, ↑TG)*** | ***↓LDL receptor expression → ↓hepatic LDL clearance; ↓lipoprotein lipase activity → ↑TG*** [5]. Hypothyroidism is a major secondary cause of dyslipidaemia |
| ***Hyponatraemia*** | Multiple mechanisms: (1) ↓cardiac output → ↓renal perfusion → ↓GFR → ↓free water clearance; (2) ↑ADH secretion (impaired suppression); (3) ↓Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase activity. ***Hypothyroidism must be excluded before diagnosing SIADH*** [16] |
| **Hypoglycaemia** | ↓Gluconeogenesis and ↓glycogenolysis; particularly relevant in myxoedema coma |
| **Hyperhomocysteinaemia** | ↓Metabolism of homocysteine → additional cardiovascular risk |
| **↑CK** | Hypothyroid myopathy → muscle fibre damage + ↓CK clearance; can be significantly elevated and mistaken for rhabdomyolysis or inflammatory myopathy [19] |

<Callout title="The Lipid-Hypothyroid-CV Axis">
Untreated hypothyroidism creates a "perfect storm" for cardiovascular disease: ↑LDL + ↑homocysteine + diastolic hypertension + ↓cardiac output + chronic fluid retention. This is why even subclinical hypothyroidism (TSH > 10) is associated with increased cardiovascular morbidity. Correcting hypothyroidism addresses multiple CV risk factors simultaneously [5].
</Callout>

#### 3. Neuropsychiatric Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Clinical Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive impairment** | ↓Neuronal metabolism → slowed synaptic transmission, ↓neurotransmitter synthesis | Poor concentration, memory impairment, psychomotor slowing; ***TFT is minimum investigation in dementia workup*** [13] |
| **Depression** | ↓Serotonin and noradrenaline metabolism in CNS | Low mood, anhedonia, fatigue; ***TFT is minimum investigation in depression workup*** [14] |
| ***"Myxoedema madness"*** | Severe ↓CNS metabolism → psychosis | ***Unusual presentation of hypothyroidism*** [1] — paranoid delusions, visual/auditory hallucinations; reversible with T4 |
| **Peripheral neuropathy** | Axonal degeneration from ↓neuronal metabolism; compression neuropathy from myxoedematous tissue swelling [7] | Paraesthesia, numbness; carpal tunnel syndrome is the classic entrapment neuropathy |
| **Carpal tunnel syndrome** | GAG accumulation in carpal tunnel tissues → compression of median nerve [6] | Numbness/tingling in median nerve distribution (thumb, index, middle, radial half of ring finger); ***hypothyroidism is a secondary cause of CTS*** [6] |
| ***Cerebellar ataxia*** | Mechanism not fully understood; possibly autoimmune-mediated or metabolic | ***Unusual presentation*** [1]; gait unsteadiness, dysmetria |
| **Delayed deep tendon reflexes** | ↓Muscle metabolism → slow relaxation phase of stretch reflex | "Hung-up" reflexes, classically tested at ankle (Achilles) |

#### 4. Respiratory Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology |
|---|---|
| **Hypoventilation** | ↓Central respiratory drive (↓sensitivity of respiratory centre to hypoxia and hypercapnia) + respiratory muscle weakness (myopathy) → CO₂ retention → respiratory failure (important in myxoedema coma) |
| **Obstructive sleep apnoea** | ***Submucosal infiltration of upper airway by GAGs → narrowing; obesity from ↓BMR; hypothyroidism is a secondary cause of OSA*** [10] |
| **Pleural effusion** | Same mechanism as pericardial effusion — GAG deposition → ↑capillary permeability in pleural membranes [1] |

#### 5. Gastrointestinal Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology |
|---|---|
| **Constipation** | ↓GI smooth muscle motility from ↓T4 effect on enteric nervous system |
| ***Paralytic ileus*** | Severe ↓gut motility → functional obstruction; ***hypothyroidism is listed as a cause of paralytic ileus*** [8] |
| **Ascites** | Rare; from ↑capillary permeability + ↓albumin synthesis (in severe hypothyroidism) |

#### 6. Haematological Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology |
|---|---|
| **Normocytic anaemia** | ↓Erythropoietin production (T4 normally stimulates EPO) |
| **Macrocytic anaemia** | Direct effect on erythropoiesis (↓DNA synthesis in erythroid precursors); also association with pernicious anaemia (autoimmune cluster) [9] |
| **Iron deficiency anaemia** | Menorrhagia (heavy periods from ↓T4 → ↑endometrial proliferation) → chronic iron loss |
| **Coagulopathy** (mild) | ↓Platelet function, ↓von Willebrand factor levels → easy bruising; rarely clinically significant |
| ***Acquired von Willebrand syndrome*** | Rare; reported association where hypothyroidism ↓vWF synthesis; resolves with T4 |

#### 7. Musculoskeletal Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology |
|---|---|
| **Hypothyroid myopathy** | ↓Muscle energy metabolism + GAG/glycogen deposition in muscle fibres → weakness, stiffness, cramps; ↑CK [19] |
| **Hoffmann syndrome** | Rare form of hypothyroid myopathy: pseudohypertrophy of muscles (especially calves) with weakness and stiffness; caused by GAG infiltration expanding muscle bulk |
| **Growth retardation** (children) | T4 essential for GH action on growth plates and bone maturation; ↓T4 → delayed bone age, short stature |
| **Joint effusions** | GAG accumulation in synovial tissue |

#### 8. Reproductive Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology |
|---|---|
| **Menorrhagia** | ↓T4 → ↑TRH → ↑prolactin → ↓GnRH pulsatility + direct ↑oestrogen effect on endometrium → heavy, prolonged periods |
| **Anovulation / subfertility** | ↑Prolactin suppresses GnRH → disrupted LH/FSH secretion → ↓ovulation |
| **Galactorrhoea** | ↑TRH → ↑prolactin → milk secretion |
| **Miscarriage, pre-eclampsia, preterm delivery** (in pregnancy) | Maternal T4 essential for placental function and fetal development; ↓T4 → impaired trophoblast invasion, endothelial dysfunction |
| ***Hyperprolactinaemia*** | ***↑TRH stimulates prolactin release*** [1]; ***unusual presentation of hypothyroidism*** — important to check TFTs before investigating pituitary for prolactinoma |

#### 9. Dermatological Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology |
|---|---|
| **Myxoedema** | GAG (hyaluronic acid) accumulation in dermis and subcutaneous tissue → non-pitting oedema |
| **Carotenodermia** | ↓Hepatic conversion of β-carotene → vitamin A → yellowish skin (palms, soles) |
| **Dry, coarse skin; brittle hair; alopecia** | ↓Sweat gland activity; ↓hair follicle cycling |

#### 10. Congenital Hypothyroidism — If Untreated

| Feature | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| ***Cretinism*** | Irreversible intellectual disability from ↓T4 during critical brain development (1st two years of life); T4 is essential for neuronal migration, myelination, and synaptogenesis |
| ***Short stature*** | ↓GH action + ↓bone maturation |
| ***Deaf mutism*** | ↓T4 during cochlear development |
| ***Protuberant abdomen / umbilical hernia*** | Hypotonia of abdominal musculature |
| ***Puffy face*** | Myxoedematous infiltration |

### Part B: The Most Feared Complication — Myxoedema Coma

This deserves its own section because it is the **ultimate emergency** in hypothyroidism.

***General features: severe hypothyroidism with hypothermia, respiratory failure with hypoxia, and coma*** [17]

| Feature | Pathophysiology |
|---|---|
| ***Hypothermia*** | Profound ↓thermogenesis (↓BMR → ↓obligatory heat production); often core temperature < 35°C, can be < 30°C |
| **Altered consciousness → coma** | Severe ↓CNS metabolism; CO₂ narcosis from hypoventilation; cerebral oedema from hyponatraemia |
| **Hypoventilation / respiratory failure** | ↓Central respiratory drive + respiratory muscle weakness → CO₂ retention → hypercapnic respiratory failure |
| ***Hypoglycaemia*** | ↓Gluconeogenesis, ↓glycogenolysis |
| ***Hyponatraemia*** | ↓Free water clearance (↓GFR + inappropriate ADH) [16] |
| **Hypotension / cardiovascular collapse** | ↓CO (↓HR + ↓contractility) + loss of vasomotor tone |
| **Bradycardia** | ↓SA node automaticity |

**Precipitants** (always look for and treat): infection (most common), cold exposure, surgery, sedatives/opioids, MI, stroke, discontinuation of T4

**Mortality**: 20–60% even with optimal treatment, emphasizing the importance of **prevention** (ensuring compliance with T4 therapy and early recognition of decompensation).

### Part C: Complications of Treatment (Levothyroxine Overreplacement)

These are essentially the manifestations of **iatrogenic thyrotoxicosis** — the mirror image of hypothyroidism:

| Complication | Pathophysiology | At-Risk Groups |
|---|---|---|
| ***Angina / arrhythmias / cardiac failure*** | ***↑Workload of heart and worsening of ischaemic symptoms*** [17]; excess T4 → ↑HR, ↑contractility, ↑myocardial O₂ demand | ***Elderly patients, those with pre-existing IHD*** — this is why we start at 25 mcg and titrate slowly [17] |
| **Atrial fibrillation** | Excess T4 shortens atrial effective refractory period → ↑susceptibility to AF; ***thyroid disease is a metabolic/systemic cause of arrhythmia*** [15] | Elderly (especially > 65 years); even subclinical thyrotoxicosis (suppressed TSH) from overreplacement increases AF risk |
| **Osteoporosis** | Chronic TSH suppression → ↑osteoclast activity → ↑bone turnover → ↓bone mineral density | Postmenopausal women; patients on TSH suppression therapy for thyroid cancer; ***osteoporosis is a precaution with thyroxine suppression therapy → calcium supplements required*** [26] |
| ***Acute adrenal crisis*** | ***↑Metabolic clearance of adrenocortical hormones → ↓cortisol and aldosterone***; ***contraindicated in patient with adrenal insufficiency*** [17] | Patients with unrecognized concurrent adrenal insufficiency, especially in central hypothyroidism |
| **Anxiety, insomnia, tremor** | Sympathomimetic effects of excess T4 → ↑adrenergic activity | Any patient on excessive dose |
| **Weight loss, diarrhoea** | ↑BMR, ↑GI motility | Often the patient's first complaint of overreplacement |

<Callout title="Overreplacement Is NOT Harmless" type="error">
Many patients (and some clinicians) consider levothyroxine a "harmless" replacement hormone. However, chronic overreplacement (even mild, with TSH just below the reference range) carries real risks: AF in elderly, osteoporosis in postmenopausal women, and accelerated bone loss in patients on TSH suppressive therapy for thyroid cancer. Always aim for the appropriate TSH target — not lower.
</Callout>

### Part D: Complications of Thyroidectomy (Leading to Hypothyroidism)

Thyroidectomy is both a cause of hypothyroidism and carries its own specific complications. Understanding these is essential because many of these patients will present to you in the endocrine clinic for long-term follow-up.

***Complications of thyroidectomy*** [1][30]:

#### Immediate Complications ( < 24 hours)

| Complication | Mechanism | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***Haematoma*** (1.25%) | Bleeding from thyroid bed vessels; accumulates in paratracheal space below strap muscles | ***Large, tense, firm, immobile neck swelling + SOB*** → significance: ***venous obstruction → acute laryngeal oedema → airway compromise*** [1] | ***Emergency: cut subcuticular stitches and stitches holding strap muscles (evacuate blood) → call seniors for intubation*** [1] |
| ***RLN injury → vocal cord paralysis*** ( < 1%) | Traction, compression, or transection of recurrent laryngeal nerve during dissection | Unilateral: hoarseness. ***Bilateral: dyspnoea + stridor upon extubation → require immediate re-intubation ± tracheostomy*** [1] | Unilateral: ***cord medialization procedures — injection thyroplasty, open thyroplasty (Gore-Tex)*** [30]. Bilateral: re-intubation, ± tracheostomy |
| **SLN injury** | Damage to external branch of superior laryngeal nerve | ***Weak voice, cannot sing high pitch*** → ***important to ask if the patient is a professional singer pre-operatively*** [1] | Usually no specific treatment |
| ***Hyperthyroidism ± thyroid storm*** | ↑Release of stored thyroid hormone into bloodstream during gland manipulation [1] | Tachycardia, fever, agitation | Beta-blockers; supportive care |
| **Tracheomalacia** | Degeneration of tracheal cartilage following removal of chronic compression by large goitre [1] | Inspiratory stridor post-extubation | Positive pressure ventilation; ± tracheostomy |

#### Intermediate Complications (1 day – 1 month)

| Complication | Mechanism | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***Hypocalcaemia / hypoparathyroidism*** | ***Only in total/subtotal thyroidectomy*** [30]; compromise of parathyroid blood supply (inferior thyroid artery) or inadvertent removal of parathyroid glands; ***most common complication, usually transient*** [30] | ***Perioral numbness, carpopedal spasm, Chvostek's sign, Trousseau's sign*** [30]; ***severe hypocalcaemia → laryngospasm requiring emergency intubation/surgical airway*** [30] | ***Acute: IV 10–20 mL of 10% calcium gluconate over 10 minutes (slow bolus)***; ***Replacement: calcium carbonate + calcitriol (Vitamin D)*** [30]; Risk: 1–4% permanent, 10–20% transient [1] |
| ***Hungry bone syndrome*** | ***Sudden removal of high circulating PTH levels (in previously hyperthyroid/hyperparathyroid patients) → increased influx of calcium into bones***; exacerbated by suppressed PTH; ***associated with hypophosphataemia and hypomagnesaemia*** [30] | Severe, prolonged hypocalcaemia despite normal or elevated PTH levels [30] | Aggressive calcium and vitamin D replacement; may need prolonged IV calcium |
| **Wound infection** | Surgical site infection (uncommon — clean surgical field) | Erythema, warmth, discharge | Antibiotics |

#### Late Complications ( > 1 month)

| Complication | Mechanism | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypothyroidism** | Loss of thyroid tissue → insufficient hormone production; ***100% after total thyroidectomy*** | Fatigue, weight gain, all features of hypothyroidism | Lifelong levothyroxine replacement |
| **Hypertrophic / keloid scar** | Abnormal wound healing | Raised, thickened scar | Silicone sheets, steroid injection, revision |
| **Permanent hypoparathyroidism** (1–4%) | Irreversible parathyroid damage | Chronic hypocalcaemia requiring lifelong calcium + calcitriol | Long-term supplementation; consider recombinant PTH in refractory cases |

***Post-operative dyspnoea differential diagnosis*** [30]:
- ***Haemorrhage***: hypovolaemic shock, laryngeal oedema
- ***Bilateral RLN irritation***: airway obstruction
- ***Laryngeal spasm***: due to hypocalcaemia
- ***Tracheal injury / pneumothorax***
- ***Tracheomalacia***: floppy tracheal wall from chronic compression

### Part E: Complications of RAI Therapy (Leading to Hypothyroidism)

Radioactive iodine therapy is a common treatment for hyperthyroidism and thyroid cancer, but its principal long-term complication is hypothyroidism:

| Complication | Details |
|---|---|
| ***Hypothyroidism*** | ***Transient: 3.5–28%; Permanent: 10–15% in first 2 years and ~3%/year thereafter*** (due to late radiation effects and lymphocytic infiltration and destruction of thyroid tissue) [17] |
| **Radiation thyroiditis** | Transient neck pain and thyroid tenderness 1–2 weeks post-RAI; self-limiting |
| **Sialadenitis** | Salivary gland inflammation from iodine uptake by salivary tissue; xerostomia |
| **Transient thyrotoxicosis** | Release of stored hormone from damaged follicles in the first 1–2 weeks |

***Key reassurances about RAI*** [17]:
- ***NO effect on fertility***
- ***NO effect on congenital malformations*** in offspring
- ***NO increased cancer risk*** in offspring

### Part F: Late Complications — Hypothyroidism as a Consequence of Other Treatments

Hypothyroidism itself is a recognised **late complication** of several medical treatments:

| Treatment Context | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| ***External beam radiotherapy to head/neck*** | Radiation damage to thyroid tissue | Common after treatment for ***NPC*** (highly relevant in HK), lymphoma, head and neck cancers |
| ***Post-Hodgkin lymphoma treatment*** | RT to mediastinum/neck + chemotherapy effects | ***Hypothyroidism is a late endocrine complication of Hodgkin lymphoma treatment*** [31]; lifelong TFT monitoring required |
| ***Post-HSCT (haematopoietic stem cell transplant)*** | Total body irradiation (conditioning) + immune-mediated thyroid damage | ***Hypothyroidism is a recognised late endocrine dysfunction after HSCT*** [32]; monitor TFTs annually post-transplant |
| **Immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy** | Immune-mediated thyroiditis (destructive) | Increasingly common with expanding use of ICIs in oncology; often permanent |
| ***Lithium therapy*** | Inhibits thyroid hormone release + organification; may trigger autoimmune thyroiditis | ***Hypothyroidism in 6–52% of lithium users***; ***TFT monitoring Q6 months on lithium*** [27] |
| **Amiodarone** | Wolff-Chaikoff effect (excess iodine → ↓organification); also direct toxic effect on thyrocytes | More common in iodine-sufficient areas (like HK) |

<Callout title="High Yield Summary — Complications of Hypothyroidism">

**Cardiovascular**: ↑LDL → accelerated atherosclerosis; diastolic HTN; pericardial effusion; bradycardia/SSS; DCMP [5][15]

**Metabolic**: Hyperlipidaemia (check TFTs before starting statins) [5]; Hyponatraemia (exclude before diagnosing SIADH) [16]; ↑CK (mimics myositis) [19]

**Neuropsychiatric**: Cognitive decline (TFTs in dementia workup) [13]; Depression (TFTs in depression workup) [14]; CTS [6]; "Myxoedema madness" [1]

**Emergency**: ***Myxoedema coma*** — hypothermia, coma, respiratory failure, hypoglycaemia, hyponatraemia; mortality 20–60%; treat with IV T4 ± T3 + IV hydrocortisone + supportive care [17]

**Congenital**: Untreated → cretinism (irreversible intellectual disability, short stature, deaf mutism) [1]

**Treatment complications**:
- Overreplacement → AF, osteoporosis, angina, adrenal crisis [17]
- Post-thyroidectomy → haematoma (emergency), RLN injury, hypocalcaemia/hypoparathyroidism, tracheomalacia [1][30]
- Post-RAI → hypothyroidism (10–15% in first 2 years, 3%/year thereafter) [17]

**Late treatment-related hypothyroidism**: Post-RT (NPC, lymphoma), post-HSCT, lithium, amiodarone, ICIs [27][31][32]

</Callout>
