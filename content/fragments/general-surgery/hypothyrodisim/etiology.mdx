# Hypothyroidism

## Definition

Hypothyroidism = a clinical state resulting from **insufficient thyroid hormone action** at the tissue level, most commonly due to decreased production of thyroid hormones (T4 and T3) by the thyroid gland.

Let's break down the word: "hypo" = under/below, "thyroid" = the thyroid gland, "-ism" = a state or condition. So literally: the state of having too little thyroid (hormone).

This is critically important to understand from the outset: **thyroid hormone is the body's metabolic thermostat**. It regulates the basal metabolic rate (BMR) of virtually every cell. When you don't have enough, everything slows down — your heart, your gut, your brain, your reflexes, your metabolism. That's why hypothyroidism is such a *systemic* disease with multi-organ manifestations.

<Callout title="Key Distinction">
**Hypothyroidism** refers to the clinical syndrome of thyroid hormone deficiency and its metabolic consequences. It is NOT the same as just having a high TSH — subclinical hypothyroidism (elevated TSH with normal fT4) may not produce clinical symptoms. The term "myxoedema" is sometimes used synonymously with severe hypothyroidism, specifically referring to the non-pitting oedema caused by glycosaminoglycan (GAG) deposition in tissues.
</Callout>

## Epidemiology

Hypothyroidism is one of the most common endocrine disorders worldwide.

### Global Epidemiology
- **Prevalence of overt hypothyroidism**: ~1–2% in women, ~0.1–0.2% in men [1]
- **Prevalence of subclinical hypothyroidism**: ~4–10% of the general population, increasing with age [1]
- **Female predominance**: F:M ≈ 5–8:1 (autoimmune thyroid disease is overwhelmingly a female disease)
- **Age**: prevalence increases steeply after age 60 — up to 10–15% of elderly women have subclinical hypothyroidism

### Hong Kong / Asian Context
- ***Hashimoto's thyroiditis*** is a growing cause in Hong Kong, likely due to increasing iodine intake (westernized diet) [1]
- ***Iatrogenic hypothyroidism*** (post-thyroidectomy, post-radioactive iodine [RAI] therapy) is actually the ***most common cause of hypothyroidism*** overall in clinical practice — more common than Hashimoto's in surgical centres [1]
- Hong Kong is generally an **iodine-sufficient** area, so endemic goitre from iodine deficiency is rare
- Congenital hypothyroidism: incidence ~1 in 3,000–4,000 live births (detected by newborn screening)

### Risk Factors
| Risk Factor | Mechanism / Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Female sex** | Autoimmune diseases strongly female-predominant (oestrogen modulates immune responses) |
| **Advancing age** | Cumulative autoimmune thyroid destruction; age-related decline in thyroid function |
| **Family history** of thyroid disease or autoimmune disease | Genetic predisposition (HLA-DR3, -DR4, -DR5; CTLA-4 polymorphisms) |
| **Personal history of autoimmune disease** | T1DM, vitiligo, Addison's disease, pernicious anaemia, coeliac disease, RA — autoimmune clustering |
| **Previous thyroid surgery or RAI** | Removal or destruction of thyroid tissue → iatrogenic hypothyroidism |
| **External beam radiation to head/neck** | Thyroid tissue is radiosensitive (e.g. childhood leukaemia treatment) |
| **High iodine intake** | Wolff-Chaikoff effect — excess iodine paradoxically inhibits thyroid hormone synthesis |
| **Drugs** (amiodarone, lithium, immune checkpoint inhibitors, tyrosine kinase inhibitors) | Various mechanisms (see Etiology below) |
| **Pregnancy / postpartum** | Postpartum thyroiditis; may unmask latent autoimmune thyroid disease |
| **Down syndrome, Turner syndrome** | Increased incidence of autoimmune thyroid disease |
| **HLA-DR3** | 4–5× increased risk (strong genetic association with Hashimoto's) [1] |

## Anatomy and Function of the Thyroid Gland

### Gross Anatomy
- **Location**: anterior neck, at the level of **C5–T1** vertebrae (straddling the cricoid cartilage and upper tracheal rings) [2]
- **Structure**: two lateral lobes connected by an **isthmus** across the 2nd–3rd tracheal rings
- ***Pyramidal lobe***: present in ~50% of people, extends superiorly from isthmus (embryological remnant of the thyroglossal duct) [2]
- **Capsules**: the thyroid has a **true capsule** (thin fibrous capsule closely applied to the gland) and a **false capsule** (the pretracheal layer of the deep cervical fascia) [2]
  - The **surgical plane** lies between these two capsules

### Key Relations (Surgically Critical)
| Structure | Relation | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN)** | Runs in the tracheo-oesophageal groove, posterior to the gland | Injury → vocal cord paralysis (hoarseness) |
| **Superior laryngeal nerve (external branch)** | Runs with the superior thyroid artery | Injury → loss of high-pitched voice (cricothyroid muscle) |
| **Parathyroid glands** | Usually 4, embedded in the posterior capsule | Inadvertent removal → hypocalcaemia |
| **Trachea** | Directly posterior to isthmus | Large goitres can compress/deviate trachea |
| **Oesophagus** | Posterior to trachea | Compression → dysphagia |
| **Carotid sheath** | Lateral to the gland | Landmark during surgery |

### Blood Supply
- **Superior thyroid artery** (1st branch of external carotid artery) — supplies upper poles
- **Inferior thyroid artery** (from thyrocervical trunk of subclavian artery) — supplies lower poles
- **Thyroidea ima artery** (variable, from brachiocephalic trunk) — present in ~3%
- Extensive anastomosis within and on the gland surface provides collateral circulation [2]

### Venous Drainage
- Superior thyroid vein → IJV
- Middle thyroid vein → IJV
- Inferior thyroid veins → brachiocephalic veins

### Lymphatic Drainage
- Primarily to **central compartment (Level VI)** nodes, then lateral cervical chain

### Histology and Function
The thyroid gland is composed of **lobules**, each containing 20–40 **follicles** — the functional unit of the thyroid [2]:

| Cell Type | Product | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Follicular cells** (thyrocytes) | T4 (thyroxine) and T3 (triiodothyronine) | Main thyroid hormones controlling BMR |
| **Colloid** (within follicular lumen) | Thyroglobulin (Tg) — the storage form | Repository of pre-formed thyroid hormones |
| **Parafollicular C cells** | Calcitonin | Inhibits osteoclasts, lowers serum calcium (minor role clinically) |

### Thyroid Hormone Synthesis — The Full Pathway

Understanding this pathway is essential because **each step can be blocked**, leading to hypothyroidism:

```
1. IODINE TRAPPING: Na⁺/I⁻ symporter (NIS) on basolateral membrane actively 
   transports iodide into the thyrocyte (against concentration gradient)
   
2. IODIDE TRANSPORT TO APICAL SURFACE: Pendrin transporter moves I⁻ to the 
   colloid interface

3. OXIDATION: Thyroid peroxidase (TPO) oxidizes I⁻ to I₀ (reactive iodine)
   — Requires H₂O₂ generated by DUOX2

4. ORGANIFICATION: TPO attaches I₀ to tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin
   — Monoiodotyrosine (MIT) and diiodotyrosine (DIT)

5. COUPLING: TPO couples iodotyrosines
   — MIT + DIT → T3 (triiodothyronine)
   — DIT + DIT → T4 (thyroxine)  ← this is the major product

6. STORAGE: Iodinated thyroglobulin stored as colloid

7. RELEASE: TSH stimulates endocytosis and proteolysis of colloid
   → Release of T4 (90%) and T3 (10%) into blood

8. PERIPHERAL CONVERSION: T4 → T3 by deiodinases (mainly Type 1 and 2)
   — T3 is ~4× more biologically active than T4
   — Type 1 deiodinase (liver, kidney): generates most circulating T3
   — Type 2 deiodinase (brain, pituitary, brown fat): local T3 production
   — Type 3 deiodinase: converts T4 → reverse T3 (rT3, inactive)
```

### HPT Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid Axis)

```
Hypothalamus → TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone)
     ↓
Anterior Pituitary → TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone / thyrotropin)
     ↓
Thyroid Gland → T4 and T3
     ↓
Peripheral tissues (T4 → T3 conversion)
     ↓
Negative feedback: T3 and T4 inhibit TRH and TSH secretion
```

**Why is TSH the most sensitive marker?** Because of the **log-linear relationship** between fT4 and TSH — a small drop in fT4 produces an exponentially large rise in TSH. This is why TSH rises early in hypothyroidism, even before fT4 falls below the reference range (= subclinical hypothyroidism).

### Thyroid Hormone Transport
- >99% of circulating T4 and T3 is **protein-bound** (mainly to thyroxine-binding globulin [TBG], also albumin and transthyretin)
- Only **free T4 (fT4)** and **free T3 (fT3)** are biologically active
- Factors that alter TBG levels change total T4/T3 but NOT free levels:
  - ↑TBG: pregnancy, OCP/oestrogen, hepatitis, genetic ↑TBG
  - ↓TBG: androgens, nephrotic syndrome, genetic ↓TBG, severe illness

### Physiological Actions of Thyroid Hormone

Thyroid hormone acts via nuclear receptors (TRα and TRβ) to regulate gene transcription. Its effects are **universal** — affecting virtually every organ system:

| System | Effect of Thyroid Hormone | Consequence of Deficiency (Hypothyroidism) |
|---|---|---|
| **Metabolism** | ↑BMR, ↑O₂ consumption, ↑heat production | ↓BMR → weight gain, cold intolerance |
| **CVS** | ↑HR (chronotropy), ↑contractility (inotropy), ↓SVR | Bradycardia, ↓CO, diastolic HTN |
| **GI** | ↑gut motility | Constipation |
| **CNS** | Essential for neural development, ↑alertness | Fatigue, depression, cognitive slowing; cretinism in neonates |
| **MSK** | Maintains normal muscle function, bone turnover | Myopathy, delayed relaxation of reflexes, growth retardation |
| **Skin/Hair** | Maintains dermal metabolism, hair growth cycle | Dry skin, hair loss, myxoedema |
| **Haem** | Stimulates erythropoiesis | Anaemia (usually normocytic or macrocytic) |
| **Reproductive** | Normal menstrual cycle, fertility | Menorrhagia, anovulation, infertility |
| **Lipid** | ↑LDL receptor expression → ↑LDL clearance | Hypercholesterolaemia (especially ↑LDL) |

<Callout title="Why Hypothyroidism Causes Diastolic Hypertension">
Thyroid hormone normally reduces systemic vascular resistance (SVR) via direct vasodilatory effects. In hypothyroidism, SVR increases → diastolic BP rises. Simultaneously, cardiac output falls (due to ↓HR and ↓contractility), so systolic BP may be normal or low. The net result is a **narrow pulse pressure** and **diastolic hypertension** — a classic finding.
</Callout>

## Etiology

### Classification by Level of Defect

The most clinically useful classification is by anatomical level:

| Type | Level | TSH | fT4 | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary** | Thyroid gland | **↑↑** | **↓** | ~95% of all cases |
| **Secondary** | Anterior pituitary (↓TSH secretion) | **↓ or N** | **↓** | ~4–5% |
| **Tertiary** | Hypothalamus (↓TRH secretion) | **↓ or N** | **↓** | Rare |
| **Peripheral** | Tissue resistance to thyroid hormone | **↑** | **↑** | Very rare |

Secondary + Tertiary = **Central hypothyroidism** (combined ~5%)

### Primary Hypothyroidism — Detailed Etiologies

#### A. Autoimmune (Most Common Non-Iatrogenic Cause)

**1. Hashimoto's Thyroiditis (Chronic Lymphocytic Thyroiditis)** [1]

This is the archetypal autoimmune cause. Key points from senior notes [1]:

- ***Epidemiology***: prevalence 3.5/1000 (F) vs 0.8/1000 (M); F:M ≈ 7:1; increases with age [1]
- ***Growing prevalence in Hong Kong*** — ?due to increasing dietary iodine intake (westernized diet) [1]
- **Spectrum**: anti-TPO positivity only (5%) → subclinical hypothyroidism (5%) → overt hypothyroidism (0.1–2%) [1]
- ***Risk factors***: older females, family history (30–60% MZ twin concordance), ***HLA-DR3 (4–5×)***, ***↑iodine intake***, ***smoking*** [1]

**Pathophysiology** [1]:
- ***Initiation***: T-cell-mediated damage to thyroid tissue → immune activation → ***lymphocytic infiltration and cytokine secretion*** [1]
- ***Role of anti-thyroid antibodies***: likely **secondary phenomena** to T-cell injury, but contribute to further thyroid damage [1]:
  - ***Anti-thyroglobulin (anti-Tg) Ab***: nearly all Hashimoto's patients; also found in other thyroid diseases and even euthyroid patients [1]
  - ***Anti-TPO (microsomal) Ab***: more specific to hypothyroidism; may directly inhibit TPO activity [1]
  - ***Anti-TSHr Ab (blocking type)***: mainly in the atrophic variant; contributes to hypothyroidism [1]
- ***Histology***: profuse lymphocytic infiltration, lymphoid germinal centres, destruction of thyroid follicles ± fibrosis [1]
- ***Consequence***: progressive destruction of thyroid tissue → ↓fT4 → ↑TSH → goitre [1]

**Clinical Features** [1]:
- ***Goitre***: usually small to moderately sized, ***diffuse, painless***, ***characteristically firm and rubbery*** [1]
- ***Atrophic variant***: predominantly TSHr-blocking antibodies → ***no goitre*** [1]
- ***Hypothyroidism*** present in ~25% at diagnosis [1]
- ***Hashitoxicosis***: a minority may initially present with hyperthyroidism due to severe follicular disruption and thyroid hormone release → subsequently progresses to typical Hashimoto's trajectory [1]
- ***Natural history***: gradual loss of thyroid function over years (minority may have spontaneous remission) [1]

**Associations**: Other autoimmune diseases — T1DM, vitiligo, Addison's, pernicious anaemia, coeliac disease (autoimmune polyendocrine syndromes)

**2. Atrophic Thyroiditis**
- Essentially the end stage of Hashimoto's or a variant with predominantly **blocking anti-TSHr antibodies**
- No goitre (atrophic gland) — the antibodies prevent TSH-driven growth
- Otherwise identical pathophysiology

#### B. Iatrogenic (Most Common Overall Cause of Hypothyroidism) [1]

| Cause | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| ***Post-thyroidectomy*** | Removal of thyroid tissue; hypothyroidism occurs in **~100% after total thyroidectomy**, variable after hemithyroidectomy [2] |
| ***Post-radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy*** | Radiation-induced destruction of follicular cells; hypothyroidism develops in majority within 1–2 years (cumulative) [1] |
| **External beam radiotherapy** to head/neck | Incidental thyroid irradiation (e.g. for lymphoma, NPC — relevant in Hong Kong where NPC is common) |

<Callout title="Exam Pearl" type="idea">
***Iatrogenic hypothyroidism is the most common cause of hypothyroidism overall***, NOT Hashimoto's thyroiditis. Hashimoto's is the most common cause of ***spontaneous*** (non-iatrogenic) hypothyroidism. This distinction is commonly tested [1].
</Callout>

#### C. Drug-Induced Hypothyroidism

| Drug | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Amiodarone** | Contains 37% iodine by weight. Can cause hypothyroidism via prolonged Wolff-Chaikoff effect (excess iodine inhibits organification). In iodine-sufficient areas (like HK), amiodarone-induced hypothyroidism is more common than amiodarone-induced thyrotoxicosis. |
| **Lithium** | Inhibits thyroid hormone release; also inhibits organification. ~20% develop hypothyroidism (especially those with anti-TPO Ab). |
| **Immune checkpoint inhibitors** (e.g. nivolumab, pembrolizumab, ipilimumab) | Immune-related thyroiditis (destructive) → initial thyrotoxic phase → hypothyroidism. Increasingly important with expanding oncological use. |
| **Tyrosine kinase inhibitors** (e.g. sunitinib) | Destructive thyroiditis or impaired T4 synthesis |
| **Anti-thyroid drugs** (carbimazole, PTU) | Excessive dosing or poor monitoring → iatrogenic hypothyroidism |
| **Iodine excess** (contrast agents, Betadine, kelp supplements) | Wolff-Chaikoff effect: acute iodine excess blocks organification; usually transient in normal glands but persistent in those with underlying thyroid disease |
| **Interferon-α** | Immune-mediated thyroiditis |

#### D. Iodine Deficiency

- Iodine is the essential substrate for thyroid hormone synthesis
- Still the most common cause of hypothyroidism *worldwide* (especially in mountainous regions — Himalayas, Andes, Central Africa)
- **Not a significant cause in Hong Kong** (iodine-sufficient population)
- Mechanism: ↓I⁻ availability → ↓T4/T3 synthesis → ↑TSH → goitre (compensatory hypertrophy)

#### E. Infiltrative / Destructive

| Condition | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Riedel's thyroiditis** | Dense fibrosis replacing thyroid tissue; rare; may extend beyond the thyroid capsule ("woody" hard thyroid) |
| **Amyloidosis** | Amyloid deposition in thyroid parenchyma |
| **Haemochromatosis** | Iron deposition in thyroid tissue → gland damage [3] |
| **Sarcoidosis** | Granulomatous infiltration |
| **Infectious thyroiditis** | Rare; bacterial or fungal infection in immunocompromised patients |

#### F. Transient Hypothyroidism

**1. Subacute Thyroiditis** [1]
- Includes **de Quervain's (granulomatous)**, **subacute lymphocytic**, and **postpartum thyroiditis** [1]
- ***Clinical course***: Thyrotoxic phase (4–6 weeks) → ***Hypothyroid phase (4–6 months)*** → Resolution [1]
- The hypothyroid phase occurs because follicular cells are damaged → ↓synthesis of thyroid hormones until recovery [1]
- Usually **self-limiting** — do NOT give anti-thyroid medications [1]
- ***Temporary T4 replacement for hypothyroid phase if pronounced or symptomatic*** [1]

**2. Postpartum Thyroiditis**
- Occurs in ~5–10% of postpartum women (higher in those with anti-TPO Ab)
- Autoimmune-mediated destructive thyroiditis unmasked by postpartum immune rebound
- Same triphasic course as subacute thyroiditis
- ~20–30% progress to permanent hypothyroidism

#### G. Congenital Hypothyroidism

Critical to detect early (newborn screening) — untreated leads to **cretinism** (irreversible intellectual disability + growth failure).

| Cause | Details |
|---|---|
| **Thyroid dysgenesis** (80–85%) | Agenesis, hemiagenesis, ectopic thyroid (lingual thyroid is the most common ectopic location — ***requires lifelong T4 replacement*** [4]) |
| ***Dyshormonogenesis*** (10–15%) | Genetic defects in thyroid hormone synthesis (e.g. TPO mutations, pendrin mutations) — ***hypothyroidism with goitre*** [4] |
| **TSH-R mutations** | Loss of function → thyroid hypoplasia |
| **Central** | Pituitary or hypothalamic defects (rare) |

<Callout title="Thyroid Scintigraphy in Congenital Hypothyroidism" type="idea">
Nuclear medicine thyroid scintigraphy using 99mTc pertechnetate or 123I can differentiate causes of congenital hypothyroidism by demonstrating absent uptake (agenesis), ectopic uptake (lingual thyroid), or increased uptake with large gland (dyshormonogenesis) [4].
</Callout>

### Secondary (Pituitary) Hypothyroidism

- Due to **insufficient TSH secretion** from the anterior pituitary
- Causes:
  - Pituitary adenoma (most common cause of hypopituitarism)
  - Pituitary surgery or radiation
  - Sheehan's syndrome (postpartum pituitary necrosis)
  - Infiltrative diseases (sarcoidosis, haemochromatosis, histiocytosis)
  - Traumatic brain injury
  - Empty sella syndrome

### Tertiary (Hypothalamic) Hypothyroidism

- Due to **insufficient TRH secretion** from the hypothalamus
- Causes: hypothalamic tumours (craniopharyngioma), radiation, infiltrative diseases, trauma

<Callout title="Central Hypothyroidism — Key Pitfall" type="error">
In central hypothyroidism, TSH may be ***low, normal, or only mildly elevated*** (the TSH produced may be biologically inactive glycoforms). Therefore, **TSH alone cannot be used to diagnose or monitor treatment** in central hypothyroidism — you must rely on **fT4 levels**. This is a common exam trap.
</Callout>

### Peripheral Hypothyroidism (Rare)

- **Thyroid hormone resistance** (mutations in TRα or TRβ receptors)
- **Consumptive hypothyroidism**: massive haemangiomas or other tumours expressing high levels of Type 3 deiodinase → excessive conversion of T4 to rT3 (inactive)

## Classification

### By Severity

| Category | TSH | fT4 | Symptoms | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Subclinical hypothyroidism** | ↑ (usually 4.5–10 mIU/L) | Normal | Usually none or mild | Consider treatment if TSH > 10, symptomatic, pregnant, or anti-TPO +ve |
| **Overt hypothyroidism** | ↑↑ (often > 10) | ↓ | Present | Requires treatment |
| **Myxoedema coma** | ↑↑↑ | ↓↓ | Severe — altered consciousness, hypothermia, cardiovascular collapse | Medical emergency; mortality 20–60% |

### By Onset

| Type | Features |
|---|---|
| **Congenital** | Present from birth; detected by newborn screening (heel prick TSH); untreated → cretinism |
| **Acquired** | Develops at any age; autoimmune, iatrogenic, drug-induced etc. |

### By Duration

| Type | Features |
|---|---|
| **Transient** | Subacute/postpartum thyroiditis, drug-induced (usually reversible), recovery phase of sick euthyroid syndrome |
| **Permanent** | Autoimmune (Hashimoto's once established), post-thyroidectomy, post-RAI, congenital dysgenesis |

### By Presence of Goitre

| Goitrous | Non-Goitrous |
|---|---|
| Hashimoto's (classic form) | Atrophic thyroiditis |
| Iodine deficiency | Post-thyroidectomy |
| Dyshormonogenesis | Post-RAI |
| Lithium / Amiodarone | Late Hashimoto's (burnt out) |
| Infiltrative (Riedel's) | Central hypothyroidism |

## Pathophysiology of Hypothyroidism — Connecting to Clinical Features

The underlying principle is simple: **thyroid hormone deficiency → everything slows down and accumulates**.

### Metabolic Effects
- ↓BMR → ↓heat production → **cold intolerance**
- ↓Lipolysis + ↓LDL receptor expression → **hypercholesterolaemia** (especially ↑LDL) → accelerated atherosclerosis [5]
- ↓Metabolic rate → positive energy balance despite poor appetite → **weight gain** (though typically modest, 2–5 kg, mostly fluid retention)

### Glycosaminoglycan (GAG) Accumulation — The Myxoedema Story
- In hypothyroidism, there is accumulation of **hyaluronic acid and other glycosaminoglycans** in the interstitial tissue (dermis, subcutaneous tissues, muscles, other organs)
- GAGs are highly hydrophilic → attract water → **non-pitting oedema** (myxoedema)
- This explains:
  - Puffy face and periorbital oedema
  - Thickened skin
  - Macroglossia (tongue enlargement)
  - Hoarse voice (vocal cord thickening)
  - Carpal tunnel syndrome (compression from tissue swelling) [6]
  - Pericardial and pleural effusions (GAG deposition in serous membranes → ↑capillary permeability)

### Cardiovascular Effects
- ↓Myocardial contractility (↓inotropy) + ↓heart rate (↓chronotropy) → **↓cardiac output**
- ↑SVR (loss of T4-mediated vasodilation) → **diastolic hypertension** with narrow pulse pressure
- Pericardial effusion (GAG deposition + ↑capillary permeability) — usually develops slowly and rarely causes tamponade
- ↑LDL + ↑homocysteine → **accelerated atherosclerosis** → ↑risk of coronary artery disease

### Neurological Effects
- ↓Neuronal metabolism → **fatigue, lethargy, cognitive slowing, poor concentration, depression**
- In severe cases → **psychosis ("myxoedema madness")**, stupor, coma
- Delayed relaxation phase of deep tendon reflexes (due to ↓muscle metabolism — "hung-up reflexes")
- Peripheral neuropathy (axonal type) and entrapment neuropathy (carpal tunnel syndrome) [6][7]
- **Cerebellar ataxia** (rare)

### GI Effects
- ↓Gut motility → **constipation** (can progress to paralytic ileus in severe cases) [8]
- ↓Appetite (but weight gain occurs due to ↓metabolic rate and fluid retention exceeding the ↓intake)

### Haematological Effects
- ↓Erythropoietin production → **normocytic anaemia** (most common)
- ↓Iron and folate absorption → may contribute to **microcytic** or **macrocytic** anaemia
- Associated pernicious anaemia → **macrocytic anaemia** [9]
- ↓Platelet function → easy bruising (rarely clinically significant)

### Reproductive Effects
- In women: **menorrhagia** (heavy periods) — because T4 normally suppresses endometrial proliferation; deficiency → ↑oestrogen effect → ↑endometrial thickness → heavy bleeding
- Anovulation → **subfertility**
- ↑Prolactin (because TRH stimulates prolactin release — TRH is elevated in primary hypothyroidism) → **galactorrhoea**, further menstrual irregularity
- In men: ↓libido, erectile dysfunction

### Musculoskeletal Effects
- Myopathy: ↓muscle metabolism → **muscle weakness, cramps, stiffness**
- ↑CK levels (due to ↓clearance and mild myopathy)
- In children: **growth retardation**, delayed bone age, short stature

### Dermatological Effects
- ↓Sweat gland activity → **dry skin**
- ↓Hair follicle metabolism → **dry, brittle hair**, **hair loss** (including loss of outer third of eyebrows — "Queen Anne sign" or "Hertoghe sign")
- **Carotenemia**: ↓hepatic conversion of carotene to vitamin A → yellow-orange discolouration of skin (especially palms)
- Pale skin: due to anaemia + peripheral vasoconstriction

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms (with Pathophysiological Basis)

The presentation of hypothyroidism is **insidious** and **non-specific** — this is why it is frequently missed. Think of it as "everything slowing down."

| Symptom | Pathophysiological Basis |
|---|---|
| **Fatigue / lethargy** | ↓BMR → ↓cellular energy production → ↓neuronal and muscular function |
| **Cold intolerance** | ↓Thermogenesis (↓BMR → ↓obligatory heat production) |
| **Weight gain** (modest, 2–5 kg) | ↓BMR + fluid retention (GAG accumulation); note: appetite is usually also decreased |
| **Constipation** | ↓GI smooth muscle motility (↓T4 effect on gut peristalsis) [8] |
| **Dry skin** | ↓Eccrine gland activity; ↓dermal metabolism |
| **Hair loss** | ↓Hair follicle cycling → telogen effluvium; brittle hair |
| **Hoarseness** | GAG infiltration of vocal cords → thickening and oedema |
| **Depression / low mood** | ↓CNS neurotransmitter activity (↓serotonin, ↓noradrenaline metabolism) |
| **Poor concentration / memory** | ↓Neuronal metabolism; if severe → "myxoedema madness" (psychosis) |
| **Menorrhagia** (women) | ↓T4 → ↑TRH → ↑prolactin → ↓GnRH pulsatility + direct ↑oestrogen effect on endometrium → heavy, prolonged periods |
| **Subfertility** | Anovulation (↑prolactin, ↓GnRH) |
| **Galactorrhoea** | ↑TRH → ↑prolactin → milk secretion |
| **Muscle cramps / weakness** | ↓Muscle metabolism + GAG deposition in muscle tissue → myopathy |
| **Paraesthesia** (hands) | Carpal tunnel syndrome (median nerve compression by myxoedematous tissue in the carpal tunnel) [6] |
| **↓Libido / erectile dysfunction** (men) | ↓Sex hormone metabolism; ↑prolactin |
| **Swelling of face/limbs** | Non-pitting oedema from GAG/water accumulation in subcutaneous tissues (myxoedema) |
| **Snoring / daytime somnolence** | Submucosal infiltration narrows upper airway → contributes to **obstructive sleep apnoea** [10] |

### Signs (with Pathophysiological Basis)

#### General Appearance
| Sign | Pathophysiological Basis |
|---|---|
| **Dull, expressionless facies** | GAG deposition → puffy face; ↓CNS activity → reduced affect |
| **Periorbital puffiness** | GAG accumulation in loose periorbital connective tissue |
| **Generalised non-pitting oedema (myxoedema)** | Subcutaneous GAG deposition drawing in water |
| **Overweight** | ↓BMR + fluid retention |
| **Yellowish skin (carotenemia)** | ↓Hepatic conversion of β-carotene → vitamin A |
| **Pallor** | Anaemia + vasoconstriction |
| **Cool, dry, rough skin** | ↓Sweat gland activity + ↓dermal blood flow |
| **Coarse, brittle hair; alopecia** | ↓Hair follicle metabolism |
| **Loss of outer third of eyebrows** | Classic sign (Hertoghe sign); mechanism not fully understood — may relate to hair cycle disruption at that specific follicular region |

#### Neck
| Sign | Basis |
|---|---|
| **Goitre** (if Hashimoto's, iodine deficiency) | TSH-driven hypertrophy/hyperplasia (compensatory); or inflammatory infiltration |
| **No goitre** (if atrophic thyroiditis, post-surgical, post-RAI) | Thyroid tissue absent or atrophic |
| **Thyroidectomy scar** | Iatrogenic cause |

#### Cardiovascular
| Sign | Basis |
|---|---|
| **Bradycardia** | ↓Chronotropic effect of T4 (↓SA node pacemaker rate) |
| **Diastolic hypertension / narrow pulse pressure** | ↑SVR (loss of T4-mediated vasodilation) with ↓CO |
| **Soft heart sounds** | Pericardial effusion (myxoedematous fluid) dampening transmitted sounds |
| **Pericardial effusion** (on imaging) | GAG deposition → ↑capillary permeability in pericardium |

#### Neurological
| Sign | Basis |
|---|---|
| **Slow-relaxing (hung-up) deep tendon reflexes** | ↓Muscle metabolism → delayed relaxation phase of stretch reflex; classically tested at ankle jerk |
| **Delayed mentation / psychomotor slowing** | ↓CNS neuronal metabolism |
| **Carpal tunnel syndrome** (Tinel's/Phalen's positive) | Median nerve compression from myxoedematous tissue swelling in carpal tunnel [6] |
| **Peripheral neuropathy** | Axonal degeneration (↓neuronal metabolism) or entrapment [7] |
| **Cerebellar ataxia** (rare) | Mechanism not fully understood; may relate to autoimmune cerebellitis or metabolic derangement |

#### Musculoskeletal
| Sign | Basis |
|---|---|
| **Proximal myopathy** | ↓Muscle energy metabolism; GAG/glycogen accumulation in muscle fibres |
| **↑CK on blood tests** | Myopathy → muscle fibre damage + ↓clearance of CK |
| **Delayed growth / short stature** (children) | T4 essential for GH action and bone maturation |

#### Dermatological
| Sign | Basis |
|---|---|
| **Dry, thickened skin** | ↓Eccrine activity; GAG infiltration |
| **Non-pitting oedema** | GAG accumulation is interstitial, not intravascular → pressure does not displace it |
| **Vitiligo** (if autoimmune association) | Concurrent autoimmune destruction of melanocytes |

#### Other
| Sign | Basis |
|---|---|
| **Macroglossia** | GAG deposition in tongue muscles |
| **Effusions** (pleural, pericardial, ascites) | ↑Capillary permeability from GAG deposition + ↓lymphatic drainage |
| **Hoarse voice** | GAG infiltration of vocal cords and laryngeal mucosa |

## Special Clinical Scenarios

### Subclinical Hypothyroidism
- **Definition**: ↑TSH with normal fT4, asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic
- Very common (4–10% of adults)
- Annual conversion rate to overt hypothyroidism: ~2–5% (higher if anti-TPO positive)
- Significance:
  - May be associated with adverse lipid profiles, subtle cognitive changes, and cardiovascular risk
  - **Treat if**: TSH > 10, symptomatic, pregnant/planning pregnancy, anti-TPO +ve with TSH > 4.5

### Hypothyroidism in Pregnancy
- Critical topic: untreated maternal hypothyroidism → **impaired fetal neurodevelopment** (especially in 1st trimester when fetus depends entirely on maternal T4)
- Complications: miscarriage, pre-eclampsia, placental abruption, preterm delivery, low birth weight, ↓IQ in offspring
- **Target TSH**: < 2.5 mIU/L in 1st trimester (ATA 2017 guidelines); or within trimester-specific reference range
- T4 requirement typically increases by 25–50% during pregnancy

### Myxoedema Coma
- **The most extreme manifestation** of hypothyroidism — a medical emergency
- Typically precipitated by: infection, surgery, trauma, sedatives/opioids, cold exposure, or medication non-compliance in a known hypothyroid patient
- Features: profound hypothermia, altered consciousness → coma, hypoventilation (CO₂ retention), bradycardia, hypotension, hyponatraemia (↓free water clearance), hypoglycaemia
- **Mortality**: 20–60% even with treatment

### Congenital Hypothyroidism
- **Newborn screening** (heel prick TSH at day 3–5) is universal in Hong Kong
- Untreated → **cretinism**: intellectual disability, short stature, coarse facial features, macroglossia, umbilical hernia, prolonged jaundice, hypotonia ("floppy baby")
- **Treatment must begin within 2 weeks** of birth to prevent irreversible neurological damage

### Sick Euthyroid Syndrome (Non-Thyroidal Illness Syndrome)
- Not true hypothyroidism but important to differentiate
- Occurs in acutely ill patients → ↓peripheral conversion of T4 → T3 (↑rT3); ↓/normal TSH, ↑/normal T4, ↓↓T3 [11]
- **Do NOT treat with thyroxine** — resolves with treatment of underlying illness
- Check TFTs only if clinically indicated in acutely unwell patients; defer if possible

### Secondary/Central Hypothyroidism — Key Distinguishing Features
- TSH is **low or inappropriately normal** (not elevated as expected)
- Other pituitary hormone deficiencies may coexist (GH, ACTH, gonadotrophins, ADH)
- **Must exclude cortisol deficiency (secondary adrenal insufficiency) before starting T4** — otherwise, T4 replacement increases metabolic rate → increases cortisol demand → precipitates **adrenal crisis**
- Use **fT4** (not TSH) to monitor replacement adequacy

<Callout title="Critical Exam Point" type="error">
In suspected central hypothyroidism with possible concurrent ACTH deficiency: **always give hydrocortisone BEFORE thyroxine**. Starting thyroxine first increases metabolic rate, which increases cortisol demand, and if the patient cannot mount a cortisol response → adrenal crisis → potentially fatal.
</Callout>

<Callout title="High Yield Summary">

**Definition**: Hypothyroidism = insufficient thyroid hormone action at tissue level; most commonly due to ↓T4/T3 production.

**Epidemiology**: F >> M; prevalence increases with age; subclinical form affects 4–10% of adults.

**Most common cause overall**: ***Iatrogenic*** (post-thyroidectomy, post-RAI). Most common spontaneous cause: ***Hashimoto's thyroiditis*** (autoimmune).

**Key pathophysiology**: ↓T4 → ↓BMR, ↑SVR, ↓gut motility, ↓CNS metabolism, GAG accumulation in tissues (myxoedema), ↑LDL, ↓erythropoiesis.

**Clinical features** — think "everything slows down":
- **Metabolic**: fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain
- **Skin**: dry, coarse, non-pitting oedema, hair loss, loss of outer third of eyebrows
- **CVS**: bradycardia, diastolic HTN, narrow pulse pressure, pericardial effusion
- **Neuro**: delayed reflexes, depression, cognitive slowing, carpal tunnel syndrome
- **GI**: constipation
- **Reproductive**: menorrhagia, subfertility, galactorrhoea (↑prolactin via ↑TRH)
- **Haem**: normocytic/macrocytic anaemia

**TSH is the single most sensitive screening test** (log-linear relationship with fT4). In central hypothyroidism, TSH is unreliable — use fT4.

**Critical rules**:
1. Always exclude adrenal insufficiency before starting T4 in central hypothyroidism
2. In pregnancy, TSH target < 2.5 in 1st trimester
3. Subclinical hypothyroidism: treat if TSH > 10, symptomatic, pregnant, or anti-TPO +ve
4. Sick euthyroid syndrome ≠ hypothyroidism — do NOT treat with T4

</Callout>
