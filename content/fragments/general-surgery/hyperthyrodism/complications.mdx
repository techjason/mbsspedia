## Complications of Hyperthyroidism

The complications of hyperthyroidism can be organised into two broad categories:

1. **Complications of the disease itself** (untreated or poorly controlled thyrotoxicosis)
2. **Complications of treatment** (ATDs, RAI, surgery)

Both are high-yield exam topics. Let me walk through each systematically, explaining the pathophysiological "why" behind every complication.

---

## 1. Complications of Hyperthyroidism Itself

### 1.1 Cardiovascular Complications

The cardiovascular system bears the heaviest burden of thyrotoxicosis because thyroid hormones directly upregulate cardiac β₁-adrenergic receptors, increase myocardial contractility, and decrease peripheral vascular resistance — essentially putting the heart into a permanent "exercise" state.

#### 1.1.1 Atrial Fibrillation

- **Prevalence**: 10–25% of thyrotoxic patients; ***thyroid disease is a systemic/metabolic cause of arrhythmia*** [9]
- **Pathophysiology**: T4/T3 increase atrial myocyte excitability by:
  - Upregulating ion channels (especially Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ channels) → shortened atrial refractory period
  - Increasing atrial ectopic foci firing rate
  - The combination of shortened refractory period + increased ectopic triggers creates the perfect substrate for **re-entrant circuits** → AF
- **Why it matters**: AF in thyrotoxicosis carries embolic risk (stroke) just like any other AF → requires anticoagulation assessment (CHA₂DS₂-VASc score)
- **Prognosis**: approximately 60% of thyrotoxic AF reverts spontaneously to sinus rhythm once euthyroid state is restored. If it doesn't revert within 4–6 months of euthyroidism, consider cardioversion or rate/rhythm control as per standard AF guidelines
- **Clinical pearl**: ***always check TFTs in any patient presenting with new-onset AF*** — thyrotoxicosis is a reversible cause

> AF in a previously healthy young person should always prompt a TFT. It may be the presenting feature of otherwise undiagnosed thyrotoxicosis.

#### 1.1.2 High-Output Cardiac Failure

- **Pathophysiology**: prolonged ↑cardiac output (↑HR × ↑stroke volume) + ↓peripheral vascular resistance → chronic volume overload → ventricular dilatation → eventually the heart cannot maintain the abnormally high output demanded by the hypermetabolic state → heart failure
- **Why "high-output"?** Unlike typical heart failure (where the pump is weak), here the pump is working maximally but demand exceeds even maximal capacity. The cardiac output is actually elevated, but it's still insufficient for the body's metabolic needs
- ***Deterioration of CVS disease by thyrotoxicosis: ↑workload of heart and worsens ischaemic symptoms → angina, arrhythmias, cardiac failure*** [6]
- **Thyrotoxic cardiomyopathy**: a form of ***dilated cardiomyopathy*** that can develop from chronic uncontrolled thyrotoxicosis; ***endocrine causes (thyroid, phaeo)*** are listed among aetiologies of DCMP [18]. Usually reversible with restoration of euthyroidism, but may be irreversible if longstanding

<Callout title="Elderly Thyrotoxicosis — Cardiac Presentation Dominates">
In elderly patients, thyrotoxicosis often presents primarily with cardiovascular complications — AF, heart failure, angina — rather than classic hyperadrenergic symptoms. This is "apathetic thyrotoxicosis." The absence of tremor, anxiety, or goitre can mislead you. Always check TFTs in elderly patients with unexplained cardiac decompensation.
</Callout>

#### 1.1.3 Hypertension (Systolic)

- **Mechanism**: ↑cardiac output + ↓peripheral vascular resistance (vasodilation) → **widened pulse pressure** with elevated systolic BP but normal or low diastolic BP
- This is distinct from essential hypertension (where diastolic is also elevated)

### 1.2 Thyroid Storm (Thyrotoxic Crisis)

This is the most feared acute complication — a medical emergency with 10–30% mortality.

- ***Thyroid storm develops in patients with longstanding untreated hyperthyroidism which is precipitated by acute event such as surgery, trauma or infection*** [6]
- ***Rapid ↑ in serum thyroid hormone levels leads to increased response to sympathetic inputs from catecholamines (adrenaline/noradrenaline) by permissive effect*** [6]
- ***Leads to cardiovascular symptoms including hyperpyrexia, tachycardia, hypertension and followed by heart failure with hypotension and arrhythmia*** [6]

**Precipitants:**
- Surgery (especially thyroidectomy without adequate pre-op preparation)
- Infection / sepsis
- Trauma
- Radioactive iodine therapy (rare)
- Iodine load (contrast dye)
- Abrupt ATD withdrawal
- Childbirth
- DKA

**Clinical features (multi-organ decompensation):**

| System | Manifestation | Why? |
|---|---|---|
| **Thermoregulatory** | Hyperpyrexia ( > 38.5°C, often > 40°C) | Massively ↑BMR → ↑thermogenesis that overwhelms heat dissipation capacity |
| **Cardiovascular** | Tachycardia ( > 140 bpm), AF, wide pulse pressure → progressing to hypotension and shock | ↑↑sympathetic drive → eventual cardiovascular collapse |
| **Neurological** | Agitation, confusion, delirium, psychosis → progressing to seizures and coma | ↑↑CNS excitability from extreme T4/catecholamine synergy |
| **GI** | Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, jaundice, abdominal pain | ↑↑GI motility + hepatic dysfunction from hypoperfusion |
| **Metabolic** | Hyperglycaemia, hypercalcaemia | ↑gluconeogenesis, ↑bone resorption |

**Diagnosis**: clinical (Burch-Wartofsky Point Scale, or BWPS) — scored based on temperature, CNS effects, GI-hepatic dysfunction, cardiovascular dysfunction, heart failure, and precipitant history. A score ≥ 45 is highly suggestive of thyroid storm.

**Management** (covered in detail in the Management section — briefly recapped here):
1. ***PTU + Hydrocortisone + Propranolol*** (initial regimen) [6]
2. ***Iodide (Lugol's solution / NaI) after ≥ 1 hour*** (block hormone release AFTER synthesis is blocked) [6]
3. ***Paracetamol (NOT salicylate) for hyperpyrexia + physical cooling*** [6]
4. ***IV fluids, O₂, supportive care***

### 1.3 Thyrotoxic Periodic Paralysis (TPP)

- ***Up to 2% among Asian patients with hyperthyroidism*** [1]
- ***Pathophysiology: ↑Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase activity + ↑insulin release → intracellular shift of K⁺*** [1] → **redistributive hypokalaemia** → muscle membrane hyperpolarisation → paralysis
- ***Attacks of motor paralysis: proximal > distal, LL > UL, seldom respiratory/bulbar muscles*** [1]
- ***Cardiac arrhythmia due to severe hypoK (mean serum K = 2.1 but can be < 1.5)*** [1] — this is the life-threatening aspect
- ***Precipitants: rest after strenuous activity, stress, SABA use; ↑carbohydrate load*** [1]
- ***Management: cautious IV K 10–20 mmol/h over 2h; watch for rebound hyperkalaemia (40–59%)*** [1] — because total body K⁺ is normal (redistributive, not depleted)
- **Definitive treatment**: control the underlying hyperthyroidism → TPP does not recur once euthyroid

<Callout title="TPP — Rebound Hyperkalaemia" type="error">
The most dangerous mistake in managing TPP is aggressive potassium replacement. Because this is **redistributive** hypokalaemia (total body K⁺ is normal), when the transcellular shift reverses (treatment of hyperthyroidism + insulin effect wears off), the replaced K⁺ PLUS the K⁺ shifting back out of cells can cause **life-threatening hyperkalaemia**. Replace cautiously and monitor closely [1].
</Callout>

### 1.4 Osteoporosis

- **Pathophysiology**: T4/T3 directly stimulate osteoclast activity → ↑bone resorption → negative bone balance → ↓bone mineral density
- **Who is most at risk**: postmenopausal women with prolonged untreated thyrotoxicosis (double hit: oestrogen deficiency + thyroid hormone excess)
- **Also relevant**: excessive T4 replacement (iatrogenic subclinical hyperthyroidism) causing the same bone loss → important in follow-up of patients on levothyroxine
- **Biochemical markers**: ↑serum calcium, ↑alkaline phosphatase, ↑urinary calcium/hydroxyproline
- **Management**: treat the hyperthyroidism; calcium + vitamin D supplementation; DEXA scan if prolonged thyrotoxicosis

### 1.5 Ophthalmological Complications (Graves' Ophthalmopathy)

This complicates ~20–25% of Graves' disease and runs an independent course from the thyroid disease.

***Sight-threatening complications include*** [1][4]:

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Clinical Features | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***Compressive optic neuropathy ( < 5%)*** | ***Oversized recti + orbital fat → apical crowding → compressive optic neuropathy*** [1][4] | ***Slowly progressive ↓vision (esp colour vision, contrast sensitivity); optic disc oedema/pallor, RAPD+, central scotoma with inferior arcuate defects*** [1][4] | ***Usually stabilises over 3–5 years but can lead to blindness*** [1][4] |
| ***Exposure keratopathy*** | Proptosis + lagophthalmos → corneal drying | ***Chemosis, punctate erosions, corneal ulcer, corneal perforation*** [1][4]; ***RFs: lagophthalmos, degree of proptosis, integrity of Bell's reflex*** [1][4] | Risk of permanent corneal scarring/perforation |
| ***Secondary open angle glaucoma*** | ***↑episcleral venous pressure and EOM swelling compressing onto the globe*** [1][4] | ↑IOP, visual field loss | Requires IOP-lowering treatment |

**Why Graves' ophthalmopathy is technically a "complication"**: The orbital disease is an autoimmune process driven by TRAb acting on orbital fibroblasts. It is not simply a consequence of high thyroid hormone levels — it can occur (and worsen) even in euthyroid or hypothyroid patients. However, poorly controlled thyroid status (both hyper- and hypothyroidism) worsens its course.

### 1.6 Neuropsychiatric Complications

- **Anxiety, psychosis, emotional lability**: direct CNS excitatory effects of T4/T3
- **Proximal myopathy**: ↑protein catabolism → type II muscle fibre atrophy → difficulty climbing stairs, rising from squat
- **Tremor**: enhanced physiological tremor from ↑β-adrenergic activity

### 1.7 Metabolic Complications

| Complication | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Hyperglycaemia / Worsening of DM** | ↑gluconeogenesis, ↑glycogenolysis, ↑insulin resistance; thyrotoxicosis is listed as an endocrine cause of DM [8] |
| **Hypercalcaemia** (~10%) | ↑osteoclast-mediated bone resorption → release of calcium |
| **Hypomagnesaemia** | ***Hyperthyroidism*** is listed as an endocrine cause of hypomagnesaemia [19] — ↑renal Mg excretion from ↑GFR and direct tubular effects |
| **Weight loss / Malnutrition** | ↑BMR outstrips caloric intake; prolonged cases → protein-calorie malnutrition |

### 1.8 Reproductive Complications

- **Menstrual irregularity**: oligomenorrhoea/amenorrhoea (↑SHBG binds oestrogen; disrupted GnRH pulsatility)
- **Subfertility**: anovulatory cycles
- **Pregnancy complications**: miscarriage, preterm birth, pre-eclampsia, low birth weight, neonatal thyrotoxicosis (transplacental TRAb transfer)
- **Neonatal Graves' disease**: maternal TRAb crosses placenta → stimulates fetal/neonatal thyroid → fetal/neonatal thyrotoxicosis; risk correlates with maternal TRAb levels [1]

## 2. Complications of Treatment

### 2.1 Complications of Anti-Thyroid Drugs (ATDs)

| Complication | Frequency | Mechanism | Key Points |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***Skin rash / urticaria / pruritus*** | ***5%*** [6] | ***Allergy — triggers release of histamine*** [6] | ***Treated by antihistamine*** [6]; usually not a reason to stop |
| ***Agranulocytosis*** | ***0.1–0.5%*** [1][6] | Immune-mediated destruction of granulocyte precursors | ***Occurs within first 2–3 months*** [1][6]; ***presents with fever/sore throat*** [1]; ***predicted by HLA-B*38:02:01 (Asian population)*** [1]; STOP the drug immediately; check urgent FBC |
| ***Hepatotoxicity*** | Variable | PTU: hepatocellular necrosis; CBZ: cholestatic injury | ***Hepatic necrosis*** [6] — PTU can rarely cause fulminant hepatic failure; baseline LFT before starting |
| ***Teratogenicity*** | — | Direct embryotoxicity | ***Aplasia cutis, choanal atresia (methimazole/carbimazole >> PTU)*** [1]; PTU preferred in 1st trimester |
| ***Arthritis / Arthralgia*** [6] | Rare | Immune-mediated | Usually self-limiting |
| ***Fever*** [6] | Rare | Drug hypersensitivity | Must distinguish from agranulocytosis-related infection |

### 2.2 Complications of Radioactive Iodine (RAI)

| Complication | Frequency | Mechanism | Key Points |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***Hypothyroidism (permanent)*** | ***10–15% in first 2 years; 3% per year thereafter*** [6] | ***Late effects of radiation and of lymphocytic infiltration and destruction of thyroid tissue*** [6] | Virtually all patients eventually become hypothyroid → ***require lifelong T4 replacement*** [6] |
| ***Transient hypothyroidism*** | ***3.5–28%*** [6] | Temporary gland dysfunction | May recover |
| **Radiation thyroiditis** | ~1–5% | Acute inflammatory response to radiation damage | Painful neck swelling 1–2 weeks post-treatment; self-limiting |
| **Worsening Graves' ophthalmopathy** | Variable | Release of thyroid antigens during gland destruction → ↑TRAb → ↑orbital inflammation | ***Moderate/severe GO is a contraindication to RAI*** [1][4]; concurrent glucocorticoids for mild GO with risk factors [1][4] |
| **Transient thyrotoxicosis** | Rare | Stored hormone released from destroyed follicles | Self-limiting; manage with β-blockers |

***Reassurances regarding RAI*** [6]:
- ***NO effect on fertility***
- ***NO effect on congenital malformations***
- ***NO effect on increased cancer risk of offspring***

### 2.3 Complications of Thyroidectomy

This is one of the most commonly tested topics in surgical exams. The complications are best classified by timing.

***Complications of thyroidectomy*** [1][6][17]:

#### 2.3.1 Immediate (Intraoperative)

| Complication | Mechanism | Management |
|---|---|---|
| ***Intraoperative bleeding*** [6] | Injury to thyroid vessels (superior/inferior thyroid arteries or veins) | Meticulous surgical haemostasis |
| ***Oesophageal injury*** [6] | Direct surgical trauma (rare) | Immediate repair |
| ***Tracheal injury*** [6] | Direct surgical trauma (rare) | Immediate repair |
| ***Tracheomalacia*** [6] | ***Degeneration of cartilage following removal of compression by large goitre*** [1] — chronic compression weakens tracheal rings; once the goitre is removed, the unsupported tracheal wall collapses | May require tracheostomy or stenting |
| ***Superior laryngeal nerve (SLN) injury*** [6] | ***SLN supplies the cricothyroid muscle which lengthens (tenses) the vocal cord to produce high-pitched sound*** [6]; runs with superior thyroid artery → vulnerable during ligation | ***Presents with vocal fatigue and changes in voice quality*** [6]; loss of high pitch; ***important to ask if the patient is a professional singer pre-op*** [1] |
| ***Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN) injury*** | ***RLN supplies all intrinsic muscles of larynx except cricothyroid*** [6]; runs in the tracheo-oesophageal groove → vulnerable during dissection | See below |

**RLN Injury — Detailed Breakdown:**

| Type | Presentation | Mechanism [6][17] |
|---|---|---|
| ***Unilateral RLN injury*** | ***Hoarseness and ineffective cough; unilateral vocal cord palsy*** [6] | Affected cord stays in paramedian position; contralateral cord compensates partially |
| ***Bilateral RLN injury*** | ***Stridor and dyspnoea (airway obstruction)*** [6]; bilateral vocal cord palsy → ***presents with stridor and dyspnoea upon extubation*** [1] | Both cords adduct to midline (6 adductor muscles vs 2 abductor muscles → ***6 adductors > 2 abductors*** [17] → cords default to closed position) → airway obstruction |
| **Transient** | Resolves in weeks to months | Tractional injury (neuropraxia) from retraction without transection |
| **Permanent** | Does not recover | Transection or severe thermal/ischaemic injury |

***Management of unilateral RLN injury: cord medialisation procedures (e.g. injection thyroplasty, open thyroplasty with Gortex)*** [17]

***Bilateral RLN injury: require immediate re-intubation ± tracheostomy*** [1]

- ***↑risk of aspiration pneumonia*** [6] (incompetent laryngeal closure)

<Callout title="Post-Op Dyspnoea After Thyroidectomy — DDx" type="error">
***Differential diagnosis of post-operative dyspnoea*** [17]:
1. **Haematoma** → hypovolaemic shock + laryngeal oedema (from venous obstruction)
2. **Bilateral RLN irritation/injury** → airway obstruction
3. **Laryngeal spasm** → due to hypocalcaemia
4. **Tracheal injury / Pneumothorax**
5. **Tracheomalacia** → floppy tracheal wall after removal of long-standing goitre

This is a classic viva/OSCE question. A patient who becomes dyspnoeic after thyroidectomy is a surgical emergency until proven otherwise.
</Callout>

#### 2.3.2 Early (1 Day – 1 Month)

| Complication | Frequency | Details |
|---|---|---|
| ***Haematoma*** | ***1.25%*** [1] — uncommon but potentially fatal | ***Usually in paratracheal region below strap muscles → causes venous obstruction → acute laryngeal oedema → risk of airway compromise*** [1]. ***S/S: large, tense, firm immobile neck swelling + SOB*** [1]. ***Management: cut subcuticular stitches and stitches holding strap muscles (evacuate all blood) → call seniors for intubation*** [1] |
| **Seroma** | Common | ***Superficial, mobile (self-limiting)*** [1] |
| ***Wound infection*** [6] | Low (clean surgical field) | ***Wound infection is NOT a recognised complication*** of clean thyroid surgery [17] (controversial — some sources list it, but the risk is extremely low in a clean field) |
| ***Hypoparathyroidism → Hypocalcaemia*** | ***Risk: 1–4% permanent (esp in cancer surgery); 10–20% transient (esp in ischaemia)*** [1] | See detailed discussion below |
| **Thyroid storm** | Rare (if proper pre-op preparation) | ***↑release of stored thyroid hormone into bloodstream*** [1] — manipulation of the gland during surgery can squeeze out stored hormone |

#### 2.3.3 Hypoparathyroidism and Hypocalcaemia — In Detail

This is the ***MOST common complication*** of thyroidectomy [6][17].

**Why does it happen?**
- The parathyroid glands (4 tiny glands, each ~5mm) are embedded on the posterior surface of the thyroid, supplied by the inferior thyroid artery
- During thyroidectomy, they can be:
  - **Devascularised** (***often due to compromise of inferior thyroid artery*** [1] — the most common mechanism)
  - **Inadvertently removed** (especially in total thyroidectomy)
  - **Bruised** by surgical manipulation
- ***Only in total thyroidectomy*** [17] — hemithyroidectomy preserves the contralateral parathyroids

**Clinical features (hypocalcaemia):**
- ***Perioral numbness*** [17], acral paraesthesia
- ***Carpopedal spasm, muscle spasms and cramps*** [6]
- ***Trousseau's sign*** (carpal spasm when BP cuff inflated above systolic for 3 minutes)
- ***Chvostek's sign*** (facial twitch on tapping facial nerve anterior to ear)
- ***Severe hypocalcaemia can lead to laryngospasm requiring emergency intubation/surgical airway*** [17]
- ***ECG: prolonged QT interval ± arrhythmia*** [1]

**Mnemonic for hypocalcaemia symptoms: "CATS GO NUMB"** [1]:
- **C**onvulsion
- **A**rrhythmia
- **T**etany
- **S**pasm (laryngospasm)
- **GO** (transition word)
- **NUMB**ness (perioral, distal)

**Management:**
- ***Fast replacement: IV 10–20 mL of 10% calcium gluconate over 10 minutes (slow bolus)*** [6]
- ***Maintenance replacement: Calcium carbonate + Calcitriol (Vitamin D)*** [6]
- Check serum corrected Ca²⁺ and PTH postoperatively [6]

#### 2.3.4 Hungry Bone Syndrome

***Definition: severe and prolonged hypocalcaemia despite normal or even elevated levels of PTH*** [6].

This deserves special mention because it catches people off guard — the PTH level suggests the parathyroids are fine, yet the patient is profoundly hypocalcaemic.

**Pathophysiology** [6]:
- In patients with pre-operative hyperthyroidism (or hyperparathyroidism), there is chronically ↑bone turnover with ↑osteoclast activity → bones are constantly resorbing
- ***Sudden removal of the effect of high circulating levels of PTH (or thyroid hormone) → leads to increased influx of calcium into bones*** [6]
- The bones "hunger" for calcium — osteoblastic activity suddenly predominates → calcium floods into bone → **dramatic fall in serum calcium**
- ***Associated with hypophosphataemia and hypomagnesaemia*** [6]
- ***Pre-op high bone turnover with sudden ↓PTH → ↑↑↑bone ossification → sudden hypocalcaemia*** [1]

**Why PTH is elevated**: the parathyroids detect the falling calcium and mount an appropriate PTH response — but the bone's calcium appetite is so voracious that even maximal PTH cannot keep up.

#### 2.3.5 Late Complications

| Complication | Details |
|---|---|
| ***Hypothyroidism*** | ***100% after total thyroidectomy*** [17]; requires lifelong T4 replacement |
| ***Recurrence*** [6] | Of thyrotoxicosis (if subtotal thyroidectomy) or malignancy |
| ***Hypertrophic scar / Keloid formation*** [6] | Cosmetic concern; surgical scar on anterior neck |

<Callout title="High Yield Summary — Complications of Hyperthyroidism">

**Disease complications:**
- **AF** (10–25%): ↑atrial excitability; always check TFTs in new AF; ~60% revert when euthyroid
- **High-output cardiac failure**: prolonged ↑cardiac demand; can cause dilated cardiomyopathy
- **Thyroid storm**: decompensated thyrotoxicosis; precipitated by surgery/infection/trauma; treat with PTU + hydrocortisone + propranolol → iodide after 1 hour; NOT salicylates
- **TPP**: Asian males; redistributive hypoK; cautious K replacement (rebound hyperK risk 40-59%)
- **Osteoporosis**: ↑osteoclast activity; esp postmenopausal women
- **Graves' ophthalmopathy**: sight-threatening = compressive optic neuropathy ( < 5%), exposure keratopathy, secondary glaucoma

**Treatment complications:**
- **ATDs**: agranulocytosis (0.1-0.5%, first 2-3 months, fever + sore throat); hepatotoxicity (PTU > CBZ); teratogenicity (CBZ > PTU)
- **RAI**: hypothyroidism (inevitable); worsening GO; NO effect on fertility/cancer risk
- **Surgery**: Hypoparathyroidism/hypoCa (MOST common, "CATS GO NUMB"); RLN injury (unilateral = hoarseness, bilateral = airway obstruction); haematoma (rare but lethal); hungry bone syndrome

**Post-thyroidectomy dyspnoea DDx**: haematoma, bilateral RLN injury, laryngospasm (hypoCa), tracheal injury/pneumothorax, tracheomalacia

</Callout>
